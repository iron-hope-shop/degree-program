Human Brains and Neural Networks

Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) and biological neural networks, although conceptually parallel in certain respects, demonstrate considerable differences in their function and structure. Both can be considered as systems of interconnected nodes (or "neurons") that process information by modifying their internal states in response to inputs (Zador, 2019). It's crucial to understand, though, that biological neurons' complex operations are far more intricate than those in ANNs.

In the context of biological neurons, input is received and processed, which, when exceeding a specific threshold, leads to the neuron 'firing' and passing on a signal to other neurons. This communication is facilitated through complex electrochemical signals, encompassing a combination of excitatory and inhibitory effects.

Artificial neurons in ANNs, however, work in a different manner. They receive numerical input data, perform a basic calculation (like a weighted sum), and then apply a typically nonlinear function (the activation function) to determine their output. Unlike biological synapses that can strengthen or weaken over time, the connection strengths or 'weights' in ANNs are largely deterministic.

On a network level, ANNs typically have a static architecture with 'layers' of neurons. Learning within these networks is achieved through the adjustment of connection weights based on the errors made by the network. In contrast, biological neural networks are dynamic and plastic, adapting their connections and intrinsic properties in a complex manner to learn and evolve. 

When it comes to learning, humans and machines follow quite different paths. Humans learn from experiences, comprehending context, understanding abstract concepts, and applying prior knowledge to new situations. This learning process involves not only pattern recognition but also the ability to understand, conceptualize, and generalize.

On the other hand, machine learning, which is the learning mechanism for ANNs, primarily relies on pattern recognition within the input data. ANNs are trained on a large volume of data, with their learning capability limited to identifying patterns and correlations within that data. While ANNs can extrapolate and make predictions based on the learned patterns, they lack the ability to understand context or apply conceptual knowledge, which is a distinctive characteristic of human learning (Marcus, 2018).
