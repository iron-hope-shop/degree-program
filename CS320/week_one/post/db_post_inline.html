Introductions and Experience

Hello, my name is Brad. I am the proud father of three children and proud husband to a beautiful wife. I currently work as a Systems Engineer III in Information Security for Walmart Associates, Inc. I am enrolled in the bachelors program for computer science with a focus in cybersecurity. I am lucky to already have a job I would consider to be my dream job; however, in order to progress up the food chain and eventually buy a Ferrari, I have chosen to upskill.

I ensure that my software and programs are correct by taking advantage of my IDE's (I prefer VSCode) linters and constantly debugging. Once deployed I also test in a development environment before I deploy to production. In software meant for larger, distributed, systems it is also preferable to create automated end-to-end testing. Software professionals test their code locally, some have integrations in CI/CD pipelines, and UI/feature testing in dev. The meet requirements by following specs provided by senior engineers and ASKING FOR HELP when there are uncertainties.

These strategies have helped me as a developer by ensuring that the vision of a certain product was carried on throughout its development lifecycle. Of course, it is organization and role specific which method of planning and communication works best for your team but there are universal guidelines (some of which I mentioned).

My experiences developing clean, correct, code have been numerous. The most testing I ever had to do on a project was when I was building and application with a senior engineer which was meant to automate account creation, termination, etc. for our company. I wrote unit tests, automated these tests via our CI/CD pipeline, integration testing, and UI/feature testing. I also had several platforms which were involved in the project so this testing extended to those platforms as well. One of them was ServiceNow, if anyone is familiar with that!

The value of testing is that you won't get fired. You should test everything you create extensively, submit it for review to your peers, and fix/test for the bugs they found. Just test! It enables you to continue working which is a fundamental building block for any sort of professional growth. :)
